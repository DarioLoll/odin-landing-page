How to learn a language

There’s no need to study, review vocabulary, or practice anything. Learning refers to doing conscious study, be it in the form of grammar rules, studying lists of vocabulary, or any other activity that engages our conscious thinking. But conscious thinking is very slow and incapable of holding at once the incredible complexity of a language.

We are receiving comprehensible input when we understand messages. The “input” part means that we are listening or reading. When receiving comprehensible input, our brain connects our experiences directly with the words that we hear associated with those experiences. These direct connections allow us to understand a word immediately when we hear it or read it, and to immediately know what sounds like correct grammar and what doesn’t, without having to think about it or translate in our heads.

While watching a video and listening, focus on the actual content of the video, not on the words, what they could mean, or why they are that way or in that order. Trying to analyze the language will also drain you very quickly and won't help because, again, language is acquired unconciously.

Do not use subtitles. Not in the language you are trying to learn, and certainly not translated to any other language. If you need to read the subtitles to understand a video, then definitionally it means that you are understanding something that is beyond what you can understand just by listening. Real-life speech doesn't come with subtitles! Even if you're in a learning environment, reading the subtitles distracts from the key activity of developing a mental image of the sounds of the language in your brain.

“Language is best taught when it is being used to transmit messages, not when it is explicitly taught for conscious learning.”

Stephen D. Krashen
